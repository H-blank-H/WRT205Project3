The Illusion of Understanding

How AI changes what it means to truly know a person.

Introduction

In How to Know a Person, David Brooks writes for readers who want to become more attentive, empathetic, and morally aware in their everyday relationships. Brooks argues that truly knowing another person is not automatic. It requires patience, curiosity, emotional presence, and the willingness to see someone as more than a quick impression.

This chapter adds to Brooks’ conversation by focusing on something he does not fully explore: artificial intelligence. AI is becoming part of how people write, learn, work, and communicate. It can help people express ideas faster, but it also raises a serious question: if machines begin helping us communicate, do we become better at understanding people, or do we slowly practice that skill less?

My argument is not that AI is simply good or bad. Instead, AI should be understood as a powerful tool that can support communication while also weakening the slower human habits Brooks values most. The danger is not that AI will suddenly replace human connection. The danger is that people may begin accepting simulated understanding as if it were the real thing.

AI Enters the Conversation

AI does not just answer questions. It changes how people write, listen, respond, and understand one another.

Human Understanding Takes Time

Brooks reminds readers that knowing a person requires patience, attention, and care. AI is fast, but deep understanding is slow.

“AI can respond quickly, but speed is not the same as understanding.”

The Work of Seeing

One of Brooks’ most useful ideas is the difference between people who illuminate others and people who diminish them. An illuminator makes another person feel seen. A diminisher makes another person feel ignored, simplified, or invisible. This idea matters because communication is not only about sending information. It is also about recognition.

Brooks’ strength is that he makes human connection feel like a skill that can be practiced. He does not treat empathy as something people either have or do not have. Instead, he suggests that people can learn to ask better questions, listen more carefully, and pay attention to another person’s inner life.

AI complicates this idea because it can imitate many signs of good communication. It can produce polite language, summarize emotions, and respond in a supportive tone. But Brooks’ definition of knowing a person depends on more than the appearance of care. It depends on presence. It asks us to stay with another person long enough to understand their complexity.

What a CS Student Knows

Eddie Chen, a computer science student, put it plainly in a conversation on April 24, 2026: “AI is really good at generating responses that sound right, but it doesn't actually understand what it's saying. As a CS student, I know it's just predicting patterns, not thinking or feeling.”

Chen’s explanation highlights a key limitation of artificial intelligence. AI systems do not interpret meaning in the way humans do; they predict patterns based on data. This distinction matters because communication is not just about producing correct language. As David Brooks suggests, truly knowing a person requires presence, attention, and emotional awareness. AI may sound thoughtful, but it does not engage in real understanding.

The Rise of AI-Mediated Communication

Communication scholar David J. Gunkel helps explain why AI is not just another digital tool. In AI for Communication, Gunkel describes AI as deeply connected to communication itself, from machine translation to natural language processing and large language models. His work matters because it shows that AI does not simply assist communication; it can actively transform how communication happens.

This matters for Brooks’ project because knowing a person depends on the quality of communication. If AI helps write a message, summarize a conversation, or generate an emotional response, the interaction may become smoother. But smoother does not always mean deeper. A polished message can still be emotionally distant if the person behind it has not done the work of reflection.

Leslie Ramos Salazar’s work on “AI-mediated communication” gives a useful name to this shift. Ramos Salazar explains that AI can shape communication between individuals by making interaction faster and more efficient. At the same time, this concept points toward a limitation: when AI mediates communication, part of the human effort involved in choosing words, reading tone, and responding carefully may be reduced.

Together, Gunkel and Ramos Salazar help show that AI is not just outside the conversation. It can become part of the conversation. That is why the issue matters for a general audience. Most people are not only using AI to complete tasks; they may soon use it to shape how they appear to others.

The Risk of Simulated Understanding

AI can sound understanding without actually understanding. It can produce kind words, helpful advice, and emotional language. For many users, that can feel comforting. However, Brooks’ book asks readers to value something deeper than the right words. He asks readers to value the human effort behind those words.

In a Pew Research Center report, Janna Anderson presents experts who see both promise and danger in the spread of AI. Anderson explains that AI may bring benefits in education, health care, and scientific progress, but the report also highlights concerns about people’s well-being and society’s future. This balanced perspective matters because it keeps the discussion from becoming too one-sided.

Anderson’s report helps show that the concern is not only personal. It is civic. If AI changes how people communicate, then it also changes how communities build trust, solve problems, and listen across difference. Brooks writes about knowing people on a personal level, but the same skill matters in public life. A society that becomes worse at listening also becomes worse at disagreement.

The problem is not that AI gives people help. Help can be valuable. The problem begins when people mistake convenience for connection. A fast answer can solve a task, but it cannot replace the slow work of learning another person’s history, fears, values, and contradictions.

Human Connection

Emotional, slow, imperfect, personal, and shaped by lived experience. It requires listening, patience, and care.

AI Interaction

Fast, useful, polished, and efficient, but limited by data, patterns, and the absence of real human feeling.

The Cost of Convenience

Jeanne W. Ross, writing in MIT Sloan Management Review, argues that many business leaders focus too much on AI as a technology and not enough on the people who must use it. Ross emphasizes the need for an “AI-savvy workforce,” which shows that human learning, judgment, and adaptation remain essential even when technology becomes more advanced.

Ross’ argument applies beyond business. In daily life, people also need to become more thoughtful AI users. If someone uses AI to help write an email, that can be practical. But if they use AI to avoid thinking about what they really mean, then the tool may weaken communication instead of improving it.

I know this from experience. In a reflection shared on April 24, 2026, I noted: “I use AI a lot because it's fast, but sometimes I feel like it replaces the effort I would normally put into explaining myself. It's easier, but it doesn't feel as real as actually talking to someone.”

This experience shows how AI can improve clarity while reducing authenticity. The AI-assisted message may sound more polished, but it lacks the version of myself that struggles, revises, and reflects. That process is exactly what Brooks values, because it reveals thought, presence, and genuine understanding.

Brooks would likely remind us that knowing a person involves effort. Sometimes that effort feels inconvenient. It takes time to write a careful message. It takes courage to have an uncomfortable conversation. It takes humility to admit misunderstanding. AI can reduce friction, but some friction is valuable because it forces people to slow down and be more intentional.

Bias, Ethics, and the Limits of AI

A serious discussion of AI also has to include bias. Elif Kartal’s study on bias in artificial intelligence systems challenges the idea that AI is automatically objective. Kartal explains that AI can reproduce bias because it is built from human data, human choices, and human systems. This matters because AI can affect decisions about people in ways that feel neutral but are not actually neutral.

Kartal’s research helps extend Brooks’ argument in an ethical direction. Brooks asks people to see others more clearly, but biased AI can do the opposite. It can sort people into categories, repeat unfair assumptions, or make decisions without fully recognizing human context. If knowing a person requires attention to complexity, then biased AI can become a form of technological diminishing.

This is why ethical AI use must involve more than excitement about innovation. It requires asking who is included, who is harmed, and whose experiences are missing from the data. A general audience should care about this because AI systems are increasingly part of schools, workplaces, health care, and public life. The issue is not only technical. It is social.

Education and Everyday Life

Education is one of the clearest places where AI’s benefits and risks appear together. AI can help students brainstorm, organize ideas, understand difficult topics, and receive quick feedback. For students who feel stuck, it can make learning feel less intimidating. Used carefully, AI can support access and confidence.

At the same time, learning requires struggle. Students do not only learn from correct answers; they learn from the process of thinking through confusion. If AI removes too much of that struggle, students may complete assignments without fully developing their own judgment. This connects directly to Brooks’ concern with attention. To understand a person, a text, or an idea, people must stay with it instead of immediately escaping discomfort.

For a general audience, this issue matters because education shapes how future citizens communicate. If students become used to outsourcing reflection, they may become less practiced at explaining themselves, listening to disagreement, and forming independent opinions. AI can help learning, but it should not replace the habits that make learning meaningful.

My Positionality

As a computer science student, I approach AI with both interest and caution. I am drawn to technology because I see its potential to improve daily life. I understand why people are excited about systems that can help with writing, coding, research, and problem-solving. Because of my background, I may be more likely than some readers to notice AI’s benefits first.

However, Brooks’ book pushes me to think about what technology cannot replace. Building or using a powerful tool is not the same as becoming a more thoughtful person. AI can help me communicate, but it cannot take responsibility for my relationships. It cannot care on my behalf. It cannot do the moral work of seeing another person deeply.

My position matters because I am not arguing from outside technology. I am arguing from within it. I want AI to improve people’s lives, but I also think that improvement should be measured by more than speed. If AI makes people more efficient but less patient, more productive but less present, then something important has been lost.

“The real question is not whether AI can understand us. It is whether we will still try to understand each other.”

Conclusion

AI will continue to become more common in daily life. It will likely become better at writing, speaking, summarizing, and responding. Many of these changes will be useful. They may help people learn faster, communicate more clearly, and access support more easily.

But Brooks’ central insight remains important: knowing a person is a human practice. It depends on attention, empathy, and the willingness to slow down. AI can assist communication, but it cannot replace the human responsibility to understand.

The goal, then, should not be to reject AI. The goal should be to use it without letting it shrink our capacity for connection. We should let AI help with tasks, but not let it become a substitute for curiosity. We should use it to support learning, but not to avoid thinking. We should use it to communicate, but not to escape the vulnerability of real conversation.

Brooks teaches that people become more fully human when they learn to see others deeply. In an AI-shaped world, that lesson becomes even more urgent. The future of communication should not only be faster. It should be more careful, more ethical, and more human.
