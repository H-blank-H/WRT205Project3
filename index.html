The Illusion of Understanding

How AI changes what it means to truly know a person.

Introduction

Think about the last time you texted someone something important and they just replied "lol" or "that's crazy." You said something real, and it didn't land. Now think about the opposite, a conversation where someone actually got it, where they said exactly the right thing and you felt genuinely understood. That second feeling is rare. And with AI becoming a bigger part of how we communicate, it might be getting rarer.

In How to Know a Person, author David Brooks makes a simple but powerful argument, truly knowing another person is one of the most important things we can do, and most of us are not very good at it. It takes patience, curiosity, and real attention. Brooks wrote the book for anyone who has ever felt invisible, or who realizes they might not be paying close enough attention to the people around them.

This multimodel version of a chapter picks up where Brooks leaves off. He does not spend much time talking about AI, but AI is now a huge part of how people communicate. We use it to write emails, come up with things to say, and even respond to friends. That raises a real question, is AI helping us connect with people, or is it slowly making us worse at it?

The answer is not simple. AI is a useful tool. But tools can also become crutches. The real danger is not that AI will replace human connection overnight. It is that people will start accepting something that looks like understanding without actually doing the work to understand.

AI Enters the Conversation

AI does not just answer questions anymore. It writes messages for you, summarizes what people said, and generates responses that sound caring and thoughtful. For millions of people, it has become a regular part of how they talk to others every day.

Human Understanding Takes Time

Brooks reminds us that knowing a person is not something you can rush. It takes time, effort, and a willingness to stay in uncomfortable conversations. AI is fast. Real understanding is not.

"The most important thing you can do for another person is to make them feel seen."

The Work of Seeing

One of the most useful ideas in How to Know a Person is the difference between what Brooks calls illuminators and diminishers. An illuminator is someone who makes you feel truly seen, they ask good questions, they actually listen, and they treat you like a full person rather than a quick impression. A diminisher does the opposite. They overlook you, reduce you to a stereotype, or just do not really pay attention. And most of the time, they do not even realize they are doing it.

What Brooks argues is that being an illuminator is a skill, not something you are born with. You can get better at it. You can learn to ask more thoughtful questions, to sit with silence instead of filling it, and to pay real attention to the people in your life. That practice, repeated over time, is what knowing a person actually looks like.

AI makes this more complicated. It can copy a lot of the surface-level things that make communication feel good, polite language, supportive tone, emotional responses. But what Brooks is talking about goes deeper than that. It is not about saying the right thing. It is about actually being there, being present, choosing to care even when it is hard. That is something AI cannot do.

When the Machine Joins the Conversation

To understand what AI actually does to communication, it helps to hear from people who study this full time. David J. Gunkel, a scholar who focuses on technology and communication, argues in AI for Communication (2025) that AI is not just a tool we use to talk, it has become part of the conversation itself. From translation apps to chatbots to the autocomplete on your phone, AI is now shaping how messages are written and received every single day. Gunkel's point is that this changes the nature of communication, not just the speed of it.

Communication researcher Leslie Ramos Salazar builds on this in AI-Mediated Communication (2026). Ramos Salazar explains that when AI helps write or shape your messages, it takes over some of the work you would normally do yourself, choosing the right words, reading the tone of a situation, thinking about how the other person might feel. That might sound helpful. But it also means less of you is actually in the message.

This is exactly what Brooks is worried about. Knowing a person is not about sending a clean, polished message. It is about showing up, with your hesitation, your specific word choices, the way you phrase things when you have actually thought about them. When AI smooths all of that out, something real gets lost.

What a CS Student Knows

Eddie Chen, a computer science student, put it plainly in a conversation on April 24, 2026: "AI is really good at generating responses that sound right, but it doesn't actually understand what it's saying. As a CS student, I know it's just predicting patterns, not thinking or feeling."

Eddie's point is worth sitting with. When you get a response from an AI, or a message written with AI help, it can feel surprisingly real. But what AI is actually doing is guessing the most likely next word based on patterns it has seen before. It is not thinking about your situation. It is not considering your relationship. It is not feeling anything. Brooks would say that is a huge difference, because real communication is not just about the words, it is about the person behind them.

The Risk of Simulated Understanding

Here is the tricky part: AI-assisted communication usually feels fine. The message gets sent. Things seem normal. Nothing obviously breaks. The problem is not loud or obvious, it builds slowly, in what people stop doing over time.

Janna Anderson, a researcher at the Pew Research Center, studied what hundreds of technology experts think AI will do to society by 2035. In her report, Anderson found that experts see real benefits coming, especially in health care, education, and science. But they also have serious concerns about what constant AI use will do to people's well-being and to how communities function. Anderson's research matters here because it shows this is not just a personal worry. Experts across many fields are asking the same question Brooks asks: if AI changes how we communicate, does it also change how well we actually understand each other? As Anderson's findings suggest, the concern Brooks raises for individual relationships has a bigger version too, one that plays out across whole communities and societies.

Every time someone slows down and actually thinks about what they want to say, they are practicing a skill that makes relationships, and communities, work better. Every time they skip that and let AI do it, they practice something else.

Getting help is not the problem. The problem is when convenience starts to replace real connection. A fast, polished message can get information across. But it cannot replace the slow work of actually learning who someone is.

Human Connection

Slow, imperfect, and personal. Built through attention, vulnerability, and the effort of choosing words that actually mean something to the person you are talking to.

AI Interaction

Fast, polished, and pattern-based. Useful for tasks, but missing the lived experience, emotional weight, and real presence that knowing a person requires.

The Cost of Convenience

This is not just a complaint about technology. Every generation has worried that some new tool was making people lazy. That is not the argument here. The argument is more specific, and it actually comes from research on business, of all places.

Jeanne W. Ross, writing in MIT Sloan Management Review, studied how companies use AI and found a common mistake: organizations spend a lot of money on AI systems but forget to develop the people who have to use them. Ross argues that AI only works well when the humans around it stay sharp, questioning it, adjusting it, thinking critically about what it produces. Her idea of an "AI-savvy workforce" basically means: do not just let the machine do everything, because then you stop growing.

The same thing applies to relationships. Using AI to help write a tough message can be fine. But if you use it to avoid figuring out what you actually want to say, you are skipping the part that matters. Brooks would say that the friction of finding the right words is not a problem, it is the point. That struggle is how you figure out what you actually think and feel.

I know this from experience. On April 24, 2026, I wrote in a personal reflection: "I use AI a lot because it's fast, but sometimes I feel like it replaces the effort I would normally put into explaining myself. It's easier, but it doesn't feel as real as actually talking to someone." There have been times I used AI to help phrase something I was struggling to say, to a professor, to a friend. The message came out cleaner than anything I would have written myself. But the messy, searching version of me that actually had to think through the words was not in it. And that version, Brooks would say, is the one that actually connects.

Bias, Ethics, and Who Gets Seen

There is one more problem with AI and communication that does not get talked about enough: AI systems are not fair by default. Elif Kartal, a researcher who studies how AI systems are built and what goes wrong with them, published a comprehensive study in the International Journal of Intelligent Information Technologies looking at bias in AI. Kartal found that bias gets baked into AI at multiple points, in the data it learns from, in the choices developers make, and in the social systems it gets used in. Her conclusion is uncomfortable: AI can actually make existing unfairness worse, not better, even when it looks neutral on the surface.

This connects directly to what Brooks says about diminishment. If an AI system has learned patterns that flatten certain people, making assumptions based on race, gender, accent, or background, then using that AI to communicate does not erase those assumptions. It just hides them. People get misread, categorized, or filtered out by a system that has no idea who they actually are. That is exactly the kind of not-seeing that Brooks is warning against.

This matters for everyone, not just people who work in tech. AI is already being used to screen job applications, grade student writing, and make decisions in health care. In all of those situations, the question Brooks asks, are you actually seeing this person?, becomes a question about systems and fairness, not just individual relationships.

Education and Everyday Life

School is one of the most obvious places where AI's pros and cons show up at the same time. Anderson's Pew report highlights education as one of the areas experts expect AI to change most, both in good ways, like making learning more accessible, and in concerning ways, like changing how students develop their own thinking. AI can help when you are stuck, give quick feedback, and make confusing topics feel less overwhelming. That is genuinely useful.

But learning is not just about getting the right answer. It is about working through confusion, making mistakes, and figuring things out on your own. If AI does too much of that for you, you finish the assignment but miss the actual learning. Brooks talks about the importance of staying with something hard instead of escaping it. That applies to school just as much as it applies to relationships.

This matters beyond grades. The habits students build now, how they explain themselves, how they handle disagreement, how they form their own opinions, shape how they communicate for the rest of their lives. AI can be a useful support, but it should not become a replacement for thinking.

My Positionality

I should be honest about where I am coming from. I am a computer science student. I like AI. I think it is genuinely impressive and I understand how it works well enough to appreciate what it can do. When a new tool comes out, my first reaction is usually excitement, not worry.

But reading Brooks changed something for me. Knowing how to build or use a powerful tool does not automatically make you a more thoughtful person. AI can help me communicate, but it cannot actually care about my relationships for me. It cannot do the uncomfortable work of figuring out what someone needs or saying something hard in the right way. That is still on me.

So I am not saying AI is bad. I am saying that even people who love technology, maybe especially people who love technology, need to be honest about what they give up when they let it do too much. The cost of convenience is real. And it does not just belong to people in tech. It belongs to anyone who has ever wanted to feel truly understood, which is everyone.

"The real question is not whether AI can understand us. It is whether we will still try to understand each other."

Conclusion

Brooks ends How to Know a Person by asking readers to take the practice of truly seeing people seriously, not as a nice idea, but as something essential to being human. That ask does not get easier in a world where AI is always ready with a smooth, helpful response. If anything, it gets harder.

AI is not going away. It is going to keep getting better at writing, responding, and sounding human. A lot of that will be genuinely useful. But the skill Brooks is describing, sitting with another person, paying real attention, understanding them over time, does not improve on its own. The less you practice it, the worse you get at it.

Using AI is fine. The question is whether you are using it as a tool or using it as an escape. There is a difference between letting AI help you say something and letting it say something instead of you. There is a difference between using it to think more clearly and using it to avoid thinking altogether. Keeping track of that difference is what matters.

Brooks says people become more fully human when they learn to truly see others. In a world full of AI-generated responses, choosing to slow down and have a real conversation might be one of the most important things a person can do.
